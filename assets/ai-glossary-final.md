# AI Terms, Explained

**Accelerator** – A computer chip purpose-built to handle AI calculations faster than a standard processor can.

Example: Data centers running AI assistants rely on accelerators so they can respond to many users at once without slowing down.

**AGI (Artificial General Intelligence)** – The concept of an AI that could perform any intellectual task a human can, not just one narrow specialty. Nothing on the market today actually works this way.

Example: When a headline asks whether AGI is near, it's discussing this future possibility — not describing the AI assistants people use right now.

**AI Agent** – An AI system that completes multi-step tasks on its own, rather than just answering a question, looping you in along the way.

Example: An AI agent could draft a reply to a client and send it once you've given the okay, instead of just suggesting wording.

**AI Assistant** – Software that understands everyday requests and carries out tasks for you, such as answering emails, booking appointments, or sorting files.

Example: You could tell your AI assistant "remind me to call Dave at 2pm tomorrow," and it sets that reminder for you.

**AI Toxicity** – Harmful or offensive language an AI might produce, typically because it absorbed such language somewhere in its training data.

Example: A well-built AI assistant includes safeguards so it won't mirror offensive language back, even if a customer used it first.

**Alignment** – The ongoing work researchers do to make sure AI systems pursue goals that actually match what people want and value.

Example: This is part of why AI companies test new models for safety before releasing them publicly.

**Anthropomorphism** – The tendency to treat AI as though it has human feelings or awareness, just because it communicates in a human-like way.

Example: A short reply from an AI assistant might feel "cold," but the AI isn't actually moody — it's simply a tool responding to a request.

**Anthropic** – A company known for developing Claude, an AI model built with a strong focus on safety.

Example: Some AI assistant products are built using models from companies such as Anthropic.

**API (Application Programming Interface)** – A defined way for two programs to exchange information securely, such as letting an AI assistant view your calendar with permission.

Example: An API connection lets your AI assistant add a new appointment to your calendar without ever needing your login credentials.

**API Key** – A unique code that acts like a password, letting one piece of software connect to another — for instance, an AI assistant linking to your inbox.

Example: Because anyone holding an API key could access the connected account, it should be guarded as carefully as a password.

**ASI (Artificial Super Intelligence)** – A theoretical future AI that would outperform human intelligence in every domain. This concept is speculative and isn't reflected in any current technology.

Example: ASI comes up often in films and futurist discussions, but it has no bearing on the AI tools available today.

**Attention** – A method that helps an AI model identify which parts of its input are most important for the task it's working on.

Example: When condensing a long email chain, attention helps the AI zero in on the actual request rather than the greetings and sign-offs.

**Augmented Intelligence** – A framing where AI and humans split the work — AI processes large volumes of information, while people make the final calls.

Example: An AI assistant might pull together research on a topic, but you're the one who decides what to do with it.

**Automation** – A process that runs on its own without manual repetition each time, with AI-driven automation able to factor in context and exercise judgment rather than follow rigid rules.

Example: A basic rule might sort emails by sender, but an AI assistant could read each one and decide which actually need your attention.

**Back Propagation** – A step in training an AI model where it examines an error it made and recalibrates its internal settings to perform better afterward.

Example: This adjustment happens during the model's training phase, long before the finished AI assistant ever interacts with you.

**Bias** – Inaccuracies or unfairness in an AI's output stemming from skewed or unrepresentative training data.

Example: If a model learned mostly from one customer demographic, its suggestions might not serve other groups equally well.

**Chain of Thought** – When an AI lays out its reasoning step by step on the way to a final answer, rather than jumping straight to a conclusion.

Example: Given a budgeting question, an AI using chain of thought might list each expense individually before totaling them, making the result easier to verify.

**ChatGPT** – A widely-used AI chatbot from OpenAI, built primarily for conversation and Q&A within its own interface.

Example: ChatGPT can help draft a message you then copy elsewhere, but it can't log into your inbox and send it — that's the kind of task a personal AI assistant handles.

**Chatbot** – Software built mainly to answer questions or chat, typically confined to one app or website.

Example: A website chatbot might tell you store hours, but it can't go ahead and book you an appointment the way an AI assistant could.

**CLIP (Contrastive Language–Image Pretraining)** – An AI model that links images with text descriptions, allowing it to describe pictures or locate images matching a phrase.

Example: This kind of technology is what allows an AI tool to find "pictures of red cars" within a folder of unlabeled photos.

**Cloud (Cloud Computing)** – Accessing computing power and storage over the internet rather than relying on a local machine you own.

Example: An AI assistant described as running "in the cloud" operates on remote servers, so it's reachable from anywhere without you maintaining any equipment.

**Compute** – A general term for the processing power AI tasks consume.

Example: Analyzing a lengthy document takes more compute than generating a short one-line reply.

**Context Window** – The amount of conversation and content an AI can hold in mind at one time, including prior messages and any attached files.

Example: In a very long exchange, an AI with a limited context window may start losing track of earlier details — similar to forgetting how a long meeting began.

**Conversational AI** – AI you communicate with through natural, everyday language rather than fixed commands or search terms.

Example: Rather than typing "weather NYC tomorrow" into a search bar, you can simply ask an AI assistant "is it going to rain tomorrow?" the way you'd ask a friend.

**Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)** – An AI model architecture especially suited to spotting patterns in images, like recognizing a face or a road sign.

Example: This kind of model is part of what allows an AI tool to look at a photo of a receipt and recognize it as a receipt.

**CRM (Customer Relationship Management)** – A system for centralizing customer details, communications, and history.

Example: A business might rely on a CRM to keep a full record of every call and email with a given client, visible to the whole team.

**Dashboard** – A screen that displays a summary of activity or status at a glance, often using charts or lists.

Example: An AI assistant's dashboard might show how many emails it handled today and which items are waiting for your sign-off.

**Data Augmentation** – A training method where existing examples are slightly modified to expand the available training material, such as flipping or rotating images.

Example: This happens during a model's development and has no bearing on how a finished AI assistant works for you.

**Deep Learning** – A branch of machine learning that uses brain-inspired, multi-layered systems to detect intricate patterns in data.

Example: Deep learning underlies most current AI breakthroughs, including the language skills behind AI assistants.

**Deployment** – The point at which software or an AI system goes live for actual use, after development and testing are complete.

Example: Once an AI assistant has been configured and checked, deployment is the stage where it starts handling real tasks for you.

**Docker** – A tool that bundles software together with everything it needs to run into a portable unit called a container, so it behaves the same on any machine.

Example: A developer might use Docker to package an AI agent so it installs cleanly on a server without compatibility headaches.

**Diffusion** – A method AI image generators use, starting from random visual "noise" and progressively shaping it into a finished image.

Example: When an AI turns the phrase "a sunset over mountains" into a picture, diffusion is the step-by-step process that builds that image.

**Discriminator** – Within a GAN (below), the component tasked with telling genuine data apart from AI-generated imitations.

Example: A discriminator might be shown a mix of real and AI-made photos and has to identify which is which, sharpening its accuracy over time.

**Double Descent** – An observed pattern where an AI model's accuracy dips before improving again as the model's complexity increases.

Example: This is a research-level phenomenon and doesn't change how AI tools function in everyday use.

**Embedding** – A numerical representation of data — words, images, and so on — designed so that items with similar meaning end up with similar numbers.

Example: Embeddings let an AI assistant connect a search for "checking in with customers" to a document about "client follow-up," because the underlying meanings line up.

**Emergent Behavior** – Capabilities that appear in an AI model as it scales up, without having been deliberately built in.

Example: It surprised researchers when large language models began writing functional code, despite not being trained specifically for that purpose.

**End-to-End Learning** – A setup where an AI model is given raw inputs and target outputs and works out the steps between them on its own.

Example: Rather than engineers coding each step by hand, the model figures out the full process by studying many input-and-result pairs together.

**Environment Variable** – A configuration value, like a setting or a key, stored separately from a program's code so it can be updated without touching the code itself.

Example: An API key is often kept as an environment variable, letting it be changed or kept confidential without editing the AI agent's program files.

**Ethical AI Maturity Model** – A framework companies use to evaluate and strengthen how responsibly they handle AI, covering areas like fairness, privacy, and accountability.

Example: An organization might use a model like this to check whether its AI tools treat customers fairly and transparently.

**Expert Systems** – An earlier style of AI that operated using extensive sets of human-authored "if-then" rules.

Example: An old expert system diagnosing a car problem might follow a rigid decision tree, whereas a modern AI assistant learns patterns from data instead.

**Explainable AI (XAI)** – AI built to reveal its reasoning, so people can understand and verify why it reached a given conclusion.

Example: In a field like finance, explainable AI might show which factors drove a recommendation, not just hand over the recommendation itself.

**Fine-tuning** – Giving an already-trained AI model further, more focused training so it excels at a particular task.

Example: A general-purpose model might be fine-tuned on a company's past writing to make its drafts sound more on-brand.

**Forward Propagation** – The standard process of an AI model taking in information and passing it through its internal stages to produce a result.

Example: Every time you send a message to an AI assistant and receive a reply, forward propagation is the basic process happening behind the scenes.

**Foundation Model** – A large, broadly-trained AI model that acts as a base layer for more specialized tools built on top of it.

Example: Most AI assistants and chatbots are built on top of a foundation model rather than being trained completely from the ground up.

**Function Calling** – The capability that lets an AI reach out to external tools and data — like your email or calendar — rather than relying solely on its built-in knowledge.

Example: Function calling is what enables an AI assistant to actually check tomorrow's forecast or pull up a contact, instead of making something up.

**GAN (Generative Adversarial Network)** – A pairing of two AI models that compete against each other: one produces content, the other tries to detect fakes, and both get better through the rivalry.

Example: Early AI-generated faces were often produced this way, with one model creating faces and the other judging how convincing they looked.

**Generative AI** – AI focused on producing new material — text, images, audio, or video — rather than only analyzing existing content.

Example: When an AI assistant drafts an email or summarizes a document, it's using generative AI.

**Git** – A tool developers use to track changes to code over time, allowing them to view history, undo errors, and collaborate without overwriting one another's work.

Example: If a config change to an AI agent causes issues, Git makes it possible to revert to the previous working version.

**GitHub** – A platform where developers store, share, and collaborate on code managed with Git. Many open-source AI projects live here.

Example: A developer might pull an open-source AI agent framework from GitHub as a starting point for a build.

**GPT (Generative Pretrained Transformer)** – A line of AI language models from OpenAI that powers ChatGPT. The term is also sometimes used loosely to mean AI language tools generally.

Example: "GPT-4" refers to a specific OpenAI model, while someone casually saying "just ask GPT" might mean any AI chatbot.

**GPU (Graphics Processing Unit)** – A chip originally designed for rendering video game graphics that also happens to excel at the math AI relies on.

Example: Training large AI models depends heavily on GPUs to manage the enormous amount of calculation involved.

**Gradient Descent** – The technique an AI model uses during training to improve gradually by making small corrections that reduce its errors.

Example: It works a bit like walking downhill in heavy fog — feeling for the downward slope and taking small steps toward it until you reach the bottom.

**Grounding** – Tying an AI's responses to real, checkable information — such as your own files — rather than vague or generic statements.

Example: A grounded AI assistant might consult your actual calendar before proposing meeting times, rather than guessing your availability.

**Hallucination** – When an AI states something false or invented with apparent confidence, so it sounds plausible despite being wrong.

Example: An AI might invent a phone number that sounds real but doesn't exist — a reason having a person check important outputs matters.

**Hidden Layer** – The internal processing stages of an AI model that sit between its input and final output, working behind the scenes.

Example: Much like the gears inside a watch, hidden layers aren't visible to you — you only see what goes in and what comes out.

**Human-in-the-Loop** – Any AI arrangement where a person checks and approves the AI's output before it's finalized, rather than letting the AI act fully independently.

Example: An AI assistant might draft a reply to a customer but hold off on sending it until you give the green light.

**Hyperparameter Tuning** – The process of adjusting settings that govern how an AI model is trained, chosen before training begins.

Example: This is comparable to setting your oven's temperature and timer before baking, rather than something you'd change mid-bake.

**IDE (Integrated Development Environment)** – An application developers use to write, test, and manage code, bundling tools like an editor and debugger together.

Example: VS Code (below) is a popular IDE — the kind of program a developer might use to fine-tune an AI agent's underlying setup.

**Inference** – The stage where an already-trained AI model is put to use to generate a response, distinct from training, which happens earlier.

Example: When you ask an AI assistant something and it answers, that's inference — the training already took place when the model was built.

**Instruction Tuning** – Extra training that teaches an AI model to follow directions well, such as "summarize this" or "reply politely."

Example: This is part of what makes an AI assistant feel responsive to your actual request, rather than just predicting plausible-sounding text.

**Integration** – A link that allows an AI assistant (or other software) to work directly with another account, app, or service.

Example: An email integration is what allows your AI assistant to read and send messages from your real inbox, not just discuss email in general terms.

**Large Language Model (LLM)** – The category of AI behind most current AI assistants and chatbots, trained on vast amounts of text to understand and produce language.

Example: When an AI assistant writes an email draft or answers a question in plain language, an LLM is doing that work under the hood.

**Latency** – The amount of time an AI takes to respond after receiving a request.

Example: A short, simple reply might come back in seconds, while a more involved multi-step task naturally takes a bit longer — just as it would for a human assistant.

**Latent Space** – An internal organizational structure an AI model builds, where concepts with similar meaning end up positioned close together.

Example: This is why an AI can recognize "car" and "automobile" as related, even without ever being explicitly told they mean the same thing.

**Loss Function (Cost Function)** – A measure an AI model uses during training to gauge how far off its current output is from the desired result.

Example: Training works to push this "score" as low as possible, similar to a golfer aiming to lower their score over a round.

**Machine Learning** – The broader discipline AI comes from, where systems improve at tasks by learning from data rather than being explicitly coded for every scenario.

Example: A machine learning system gets better at spotting patterns the more examples it sees, much like a person improving with practice.

**Meta Prompt / System Prompt** – Instructions given to an AI behind the scenes, before any conversation starts, that shape its role, tone, and limits.

Example: A system prompt might tell an AI assistant to always confirm before sending an email, quietly guiding its behavior throughout.

**Mixture of Experts** – An AI architecture made up of several smaller, specialized models, with a routing system deciding which one (or ones) should handle a given input.

Example: It works a bit like a general practitioner referring you to the right specialist — except the AI does this instantly and internally.

**Model** – A program trained to detect patterns in data, essentially a mathematical stand-in for some real-world process.

Example: A weather-prediction system, a translation tool, and the language engine behind an AI assistant are all "models" in this sense.

**Multi-Agent Orchestration** – Several AI agents collaborating on different parts of one larger task, working as a coordinated team.

Example: One agent might gather research, another draft a summary, and a third review the result — all coordinated automatically.

**Multimodal Model** – An AI capable of handling more than one kind of input at once, such as text and images together.

Example: You could show a multimodal AI assistant a photo of a receipt, and it can read the text on it alongside your written request.

**Natural Language Processing (NLP)** – The branch of AI dedicated to understanding and producing human language, whether typed or spoken.

Example: NLP is what allows you to type a request to an AI assistant in plain language and get back a sensible response, with no special commands needed.

**NeRF (Neural Radiance Fields)** – A method for building a 3D scene out of a collection of ordinary 2D photographs.

Example: Taking several photos of a room from different angles and having an AI construct a 3D model you could explore relies on this technique.

**Neural Network** – The foundational structure behind most modern AI: layers of connected "nodes" loosely modeled on neurons in the brain.

Example: Neural networks form the underlying architecture of language models and most AI tools in use today.

**npm (Node Package Manager)** – A tool developers use to download and manage reusable bits of code ("packages") written by others, avoiding the need to build everything from scratch.

Example: Setting up an AI agent framework, a developer might use npm to install it and its required components with one command.

**Objective Function** – The target an AI model works to maximize or minimize during training — essentially, its goal.

Example: This is closely related to a loss function — both describe what the training process is aiming for, just framed as something to push up or down.

**OpenClaw** – An open-source framework for building AI agents capable of taking real action on a computer — like handling files or sending messages — rather than just chatting.

Example: A framework such as OpenClaw serves as the "engine" for some AI assistant products; how it's set up and secured determines whether it's safe and reliable day to day.

**Open-Source** – Software whose code is openly available for anyone to view, use, or modify, in contrast to proprietary software whose code stays private to its creator.

Example: Open-source AI frameworks offer flexibility and transparency, but still require careful setup and security to be dependable.

**Overfitting** – When an AI model learns its training data too precisely, including irrelevant quirks, so it performs well on familiar examples but poorly on new ones.

Example: It's similar to a student who memorizes specific practice questions instead of the underlying concept, then struggles when the real exam phrases things differently.

**Parameters** – The internal values an AI model adjusts as it learns, comparable to countless small dials calibrated during training.

Example: When a model's size is described as "70 billion parameters," this is what's being counted — a rough gauge of its scale.

**Personal CRM** – A tool that keeps track of your contacts, past conversations, and any pending follow-ups for each relationship.

Example: A personal CRM might prompt you to follow up with a client next week, even if you never set a reminder yourself.

**Personalized / Custom AI** – An AI assistant tailored to your specific situation — your inbox, schedule, and contacts — instead of a generic, one-size-fits-all tool.

Example: A personalized AI assistant might recognize which clients are top priority and handle their messages differently from routine inquiries.

**Plugin** *(see Skill / Plugin, below)*

**Pre-training** – The initial, broad phase of training an AI model on large amounts of general data, before any task-specific fine-tuning happens.

Example: This resembles general schooling before specializing in a career — building a wide foundation of knowledge first.

**Prompt** – The request or instruction you give an AI, such as "summarize this document" or "find three flights to Chicago."

Example: With a well-configured AI assistant, there's no need for special phrasing — you can ask the way you'd ask a colleague.

**Prompt Defense** – Built-in protections that stop an AI from being manipulated into producing harmful, false, or off-brand content.

Example: A prompt defense rule might instruct an AI to say "I'm not sure" rather than guess when it lacks reliable information.

**Prompt Engineering** – The practice of phrasing requests to an AI in ways that produce more accurate or useful results.

Example: This is something that can be handled during setup, so your AI assistant understands your natural way of asking for things without any special wording on your part.

**RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)** – A technique where an AI checks your own documents before responding, instead of relying only on what it learned during training.

Example: Rather than guessing at a company's return policy, an AI using RAG could pull up the actual policy document and answer from that.

**Reasoning Models** – AI models built to work through complicated problems in multiple steps before producing a final answer.

Example: These models tend to do better with logic-heavy or multi-step tasks, like walking through a multi-part budget calculation.

**Red-Teaming** – Intentionally probing an AI system for weaknesses by trying to make it fail or misbehave, in order to catch issues early.

Example: It's similar to a security firm being hired to attempt a break-in, so vulnerabilities are found before a real intruder finds them.

**Regularization** – A training technique that helps prevent an AI model from overfitting, steering it toward broader patterns that generalize better.

Example: It's like advising a student to understand the concept rather than memorize answers, so they're prepared for unfamiliar questions too.

**Reinforcement Learning** – A training method where an AI improves through trial and error, earning rewards for good results and corrections for poor ones.

Example: It works much like training a pet with treats — through repeated attempts, the AI learns which actions lead to better outcomes.

**Repository (Repo)** – A storage space for a project's code and files, often managed with Git, that can be shared or worked on collaboratively.

Example: An open-source AI agent framework usually has its code held in a public repository on GitHub for anyone to view or download.

**RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback)** – A training approach in which people review AI outputs and indicate which are better, with the AI adjusting based on that input.

Example: This is part of how AI chat tools are shaped to be more helpful and natural-sounding rather than merely technically correct.

**Safety (AI Safety)** – The field dedicated to preventing harm, misuse, or accidents from AI systems, ensuring they behave reliably and consistently with human values.

Example: AI safety practices are part of why companies build in checks and limits before releasing tools to the public.

**Sentiment Analysis** – AI that reads text and identifies the emotional tone behind it, such as positive, negative, or frustrated.

Example: An AI assistant handling customer messages might use this to flag an upset customer for your personal attention rather than a standard reply.

**Server** – A computer that stays on and connected continuously, running software so it's available whenever it's needed.

Example: An AI assistant that operates around the clock typically runs on a server rather than your personal laptop.

**Singularity** – A hypothetical point where AI progress accelerates so dramatically that it becomes unpredictable and reshapes society. This remains speculative and isn't a feature of current AI.

Example: The singularity is a familiar theme in science fiction, but it's not something businesses need to consider when using AI tools today.

**Skill / Plugin** – An add-on that gives an AI assistant a new specific capability it didn't have on its own, like checking the weather or reading a particular file type.

Example: An AI assistant might have a calendar skill for booking appointments and a separate skill for logging expenses, each covering a different kind of task.

**Supervised Learning** – Training an AI model using examples that already come paired with correct answers, like a textbook with an answer key included.

Example: Most practical AI applications depend on supervised learning at some stage of development.

**Symbolic Artificial Intelligence** – An older AI approach based on explicit symbols and logical rules rather than learning from data.

Example: This approach is mostly of historical interest now, though some modern systems blend it with newer techniques.

**Telegram** – A messaging app, similar to WhatsApp or standard texting, that some AI assistants use as a channel for direct communication with users.

Example: If your assistant operates this way, Telegram is just the "front door" you use to talk to it — the AI isn't part of Telegram itself.

**Temperature (AI Temperature)** – A setting that adjusts how predictable versus creative an AI's output tends to be.

Example: A lower temperature produces steadier, more consistent answers suited to factual tasks, while a higher one yields more varied, imaginative output good for brainstorming.

**TensorFlow** – A free, open-source toolkit from Google that developers use to build and train AI models.

Example: This is a behind-the-scenes tool for AI builders, much like how you don't need to know which tools a contractor used to build your house.

**Terminal / Command Line** – A text-based interface for instructing a computer by typing commands instead of clicking through menus.

Example: A developer might use the terminal to launch an AI agent on a server — a step a non-technical user would never need to perform themselves.

**Token** – The basic unit of text an AI processes, roughly equal to three-quarters of a word on average.

Example: A longer word like "understanding" might be broken into a couple of tokens — relevant mainly for context window limits and AI service pricing, not daily use.

**Toxicity** *(see AI Toxicity)*

**TPU (Tensor Processing Unit)** – A chip designed by Google specifically to handle AI workloads efficiently, comparable to a GPU.

Example: TPUs are infrastructure used behind the scenes by companies running AI at large scale.

**Training Data** – The set of examples an AI model learns from during its training process.

Example: This is akin to the textbooks and practice problems a student studies before an exam — better quality data tends to mean better performance.

**Transfer Learning** – When skills an AI model picked up from one task carry over as a head start on a different, related task.

Example: It's similar to how learning one musical instrument makes picking up a second one easier, since some abilities transfer.

**Transformer** – A model architecture introduced in 2017 that's particularly effective at understanding language by weighing how words in a sentence relate to one another.

Example: The transformer is the design underlying GPT and most current AI language tools — essentially their shared blueprint.

**Transparency** – Being open about how an AI system operates, what data it relies on, and making clear to users when they're interacting with AI.

Example: A transparent AI product would let you know upfront that you're talking with AI rather than a person, and explain how your data is handled.

**Underfitting** – When an AI model is too simplistic to pick up on real patterns in the data, leading to poor results even on training examples.

Example: This is comparable to a student who hasn't studied enough to grasp even the basics, let alone tackle harder material.

**Unsupervised Learning** – Training an AI model on data with no labeled answers, leaving it to discover structure and patterns independently.

Example: It's similar to handing someone a box of mixed items and asking them to organize it into sensible groups without any guidance on categories.

**Validation Data** – A separate batch of examples used to evaluate how well an AI model is learning, ahead of final testing.

Example: Think of it as a practice exam used to check progress and adjust study habits before the real test.

**VS Code (Visual Studio Code)** – A free, widely-used code editor from Microsoft for writing and editing code.

Example: A developer might use VS Code to write or adjust the configuration files that shape how an AI agent behaves.

**Webhook** – An automated message one program sends to another the moment something happens, removing the need for manual checking.

Example: A webhook could alert your AI assistant the instant a new form is submitted, so it can begin working on it immediately rather than checking periodically.

**Workflow** – A sequence of steps that together make up a larger task, often spanning multiple actions or tools.

Example: An AI assistant's workflow for a new customer inquiry might be: read the message, check for similar past cases, draft a reply, then flag it for your approval.

**Zero Data Retention** – A policy where information shared with an AI isn't stored afterward — it's used to produce a response, then discarded.

Example: This is especially relevant when an AI assistant handles sensitive details like emails or financial information, since it limits how long that data exists anywhere.

**Zero-Shot Learning** – An AI model successfully performing a task it was never specifically trained or shown examples for, drawing on general understanding instead.

Example: It's like someone who's never played a particular board game but, because they understand games broadly, can figure out the rules and play reasonably well on the first try.
