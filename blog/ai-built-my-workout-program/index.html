I Stopped Programming My Own Workouts. My Agent Does It Now.

Jack Raney · June 25, 2026 · 4 min read

I was spending the first five minutes of every session figuring out what I should actually be doing. Six days a week, that's thirty minutes of mental energy spent before I even touch a weight. It wasn't a training problem, it was a decision fatigue problem. So I fixed it.

It Starts Before I Wake Up

Every weekday at 6 AM and noon, an AI agent (a program that runs automatically in the background, makes decisions, and takes action without me touching it) generates my workout and pushes it directly to Hevy, the app I use to track my training. By the time I'm in the car, the session is already waiting on my phone. It knows my training schedule, my program, and exactly what I should lift and why.

It Knows My History Better Than I Do

Before every session, the system pulls 60 days of workout history from Hevy and builds a performance profile for every exercise I've done: my 60-day peak, what I lifted last time, whether I've been progressing or stalling. Pulling from Hevy means it connects directly to the app through a software link called an API, which lets it read my full training history automatically. No manual exports, no copying numbers.

From that data, it computes target weights before the AI ever gets involved. Hit all your reps last session? You're going up. Stalled at the same weight three times in a row without ever hitting your rep target? It swaps you to a similar exercise to break the pattern. Coming back after travel or a surf trip? It starts you at a calculated comeback weight so you don't crash into a wall on day one.

The AI doesn't guess. The math runs first, and the AI builds the session around it.

It Pays Attention to the Whole Picture

This is the part most apps miss entirely. If I surfed the day before, the session gets dialed back. Poor sleep logged? Weights come down 5–10 percent. Well-rested and moving well in warm-ups? There's room to push above the program.

I play golf seriously, so every afternoon session includes at least one exercise targeting rotational power or hip stability, movements that carry over directly to the course. And if I have an injury flag active, any movement that could aggravate it is automatically removed before a single exercise is selected. No exceptions, no workarounds.

The system doesn't just know what the program says. It knows what's actually going on.

It Travels With Me

When I fly somewhere for golf, the agent reads the event from my Google Calendar (the same calendar app most people already use), figures out I'm traveling, switches my equipment profile to match the gym at my destination, and adjusts every scheduled session to fire at the right local time. An equipment profile is just a list of what's available: full rack, cables, dumbbells, or bodyweight only. At a hotel, it automatically shifts to exercises that don't need machines. When I get home, it switches back. I've never manually updated a setting for travel. It just handles it.

The Night Before

At 9 PM, I get a message through Telegram (a free messaging app, similar to texting) with tomorrow's gear checklist: what to pack, what's already in the bag, and nutrition timing for the morning session. It's a small touch, but it removes one more decision from the morning.

What Actually Changed

I don't make training decisions anymore. The system handles them, and I just show up and lift. The session is built from everything the system knows: performance history, sleep, cardio load, travel, injury, sport schedule. It's already in my pocket before I leave the house.

Most apps log what you did. This one decides what you should do next.

This Is One Piece of Something Larger

The workout agent is one layer of a personal AI system that runs my day: morning briefing, email, contacts, travel, finance, training, all of it coordinated through the same system.
