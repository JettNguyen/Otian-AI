I Got Socially Engineered by a Fake Google Call. My AI Agent Helped Me Stop What Came Next.

Jack Raney · June 23, 2026 · 9 min read

On Sunday night, I received an email from Coinbase notifying me that someone was attempting to log into my account. A few seconds later, two separate 2FA codes arrived on my phone — neither of which I had requested.

My stomach dropped.

Less than an hour earlier, I had gotten off the phone with someone I believed was a Google support representative. The call came from Google's actual outbound number. The caller knew enough about Google's recovery process to sound completely legitimate, and by the end of the conversation I was convinced I had just helped secure my account from an attacker. Instead, I had handed control of it to one.

The realization hit fast. I opened Gmail to investigate — I wanted to find the security alerts, retrace what had happened, and understand what the attacker was doing. Except the emails weren't there. The Coinbase alerts that should have been sitting in my inbox had completely disappeared.

At that moment I knew this wasn't just a compromised password. Someone had access to my account and was actively covering their tracks. That's when I opened a session with my AI agent, and what followed felt less like troubleshooting and more like running a full incident response operation.

The Phone Call

The attack started with a call that appeared to come directly from Google. My phone displayed their real outbound number, and an automated voice informed me that someone was attempting to change the recovery email address on my account. I was instructed to press a button to reject the request and was immediately transferred to a live representative.

The caller sounded credible. He claimed a hacker was actively trying to compromise my account and that he needed my help deploying a security firewall to stop it. Looking back, the script was brilliant because it created urgency while making me feel like I was already the victim of an attack in progress — when in reality, the attack was the phone call.

Over the next eighteen minutes, he walked me through what appeared to be legitimate Google recovery workflows, coaching me to read verification codes that were actually granting him access. Every email I received looked authentic because it was authentic — Google was sending them. I just completely misunderstood what they were being used for.

By the end of the call, the attacker had established a recovery path, initiated Google's account recovery process, and gained administrative access to my Gmail. He had changed my password and created multiple persistence mechanisms designed to survive even if I eventually figured out what had happened. When we hung up, I thought my account had been secured. So did he.

The Coinbase Alert

Roughly forty-five minutes later, the plan started to unravel. Having gained access to my inbox, the attacker began searching for valuable targets, and Coinbase quickly moved to the top of the list. When he attempted to access the account and trigger recovery workflows, it set off multiple security notifications and two-factor authentication requests — codes that were sent directly to my physical phone. Unlike during the Google call, there was no one on the line coaching me through what to do with them. The attacker never received the codes, Coinbase flagged the suspicious activity and automatically froze the account, and my assets stayed safe.

But something still didn't add up. If Coinbase had been sending alerts, where were the emails?

The Audit

This is where the AI agent became invaluable. Like most people in that situation, I was focused on the obvious things — passwords, devices, login history, active sessions. The AI agent wasn't. Instead of asking what devices were connected, it started asking a different set of questions entirely.

What passkeys exist? What app passwords exist? What recovery relationships exist? What filters exist? What forwarding rules exist?

It wasn't looking for evidence of the attack. It was looking for evidence that the attacker planned to come back. That distinction turned out to be everything.

The agent directed me into a section of Gmail settings I hadn't touched in years: Filters and Blocked Addresses. That's where we found it. The attacker had created a series of custom rules targeting financial institutions and crypto-related services — any email containing keywords like "Coinbase" or "Wells Fargo" was automatically marked as read and archived before it ever reached my inbox.

The alerts weren't missing. They were being hidden.

I remember sitting there staring at the screen as it clicked into place. This wasn't a smash-and-grab operation. The attacker was building a blindfold — a notification blackout where security alerts, fraud warnings, withdrawal confirmations, and password reset emails would quietly vanish while everything looked completely normal from my end. Without the AI agent directing me to those filters, I genuinely don't know how long it would have taken me to find them on my own. We deleted every malicious rule one by one, and the blackout ended immediately.

Finding the Backdoors

The filters weren't the only thing left behind. As the audit continued, the agent helped me methodically work through every possible persistence mechanism in the account, and that's when we found an unauthorized passkey — particularly concerning because a passkey isn't just another password. It's designed to provide durable authentication, and if left in place it could have served as a re-entry point even after everything else was changed. We removed it. Then we found an app password that had been generated during the attack, the kind of token used by external applications to maintain access without repeatedly prompting for credentials. We removed that too. After that, we invalidated all active sessions, signed every device out globally, and removed the malicious recovery relationship that had been established during the phone call.

By the time we finished, every known backdoor had been identified and closed. The attacker had lost the front door, the side door, and every spare key hidden under the mat.

The Ghost

The next day brought one final scare. While reviewing Google's device dashboard, I noticed a generic Android device that had been authorized months earlier but appeared to show a recent sync event. After everything that had already happened, it looked immediately suspicious, and I sent the details to the AI agent.

Within minutes it had reconstructed the timeline and pointed out something I had completely missed: the behavior didn't match an active attacker — it matched Google's own cleanup process. When passwords are reset and tokens are revoked, Google's infrastructure reaches out to previously authorized devices to terminate their sessions. The sync event I was seeing was almost certainly an old household device responding to that revocation request. The device wasn't waking up. It was being kicked out. The ghost wasn't evidence of a new compromise; it was evidence the cleanup had worked.

Building the Fortress

By that point the account was secure, but secure and resilient aren't the same thing. The attacker had successfully reached my Gmail, and I wasn't interested in relying on luck if it ever happened again. We disabled SMS-based authentication, migrated to stronger methods, and added a physical YubiKey as an additional layer of protection.

What surprised me most was learning that Google applies a built-in delay before newly added hardware security keys can become recovery mechanisms. At first it felt inconvenient, but it makes perfect sense — if an attacker ever manages to add their own key to an account, the legitimate owner has a window to discover and remove it before it becomes a usable backdoor. The system is designed to protect against exactly the kind of attack I had just lived through.

What I Learned

The attacker successfully got into my Gmail. What he didn't get was anything else — not Coinbase, not my financial accounts, not my assets. And looking back, the reason wasn't that I handled everything perfectly under pressure, because I didn't. I was stressed, frustrated, and trying to make sense of something evolving faster than I could follow.

The reason the attack stopped where it did was that the investigation was better than the attack. While I was focused on the obvious signs of compromise, the AI agent was focused on persistence mechanisms, hidden rules, audit trails, recovery relationships, and backdoors — approaching the situation like an incident response analyst rather than a panicked account owner.

That's what stood out to me most. The value wasn't that it knew how to reset a password. The value was that it knew where to look after the password had already been changed. In a world where cyberattacks are increasingly psychological rather than technical, that kind of operational thinking may end up being one of the most important uses of AI there is.

The attacker got into Gmail. The AI agent made sure they never got any further.
