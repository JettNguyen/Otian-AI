Trust

The Question You’re Actually Asking

You read that your data stays on your machine. Then you noticed Archie uses Claude. So — does Anthropic read your email? Here is the whole answer, including the parts that don’t flatter us.

The Awkward Part, First

“You say my data stays with me. But Archie uses Claude. So Anthropic sees my emails.”

Partly, yes — and we’d rather say it than have you find it. When your agent reads an email so it can summarize it, the text of that email goes to an AI model. That is what an AI agent is. Anyone who tells you their agent thinks without sending anything anywhere is selling you something that does not work.

So the honest question isn’t does anything leave. It’s whose account it goes under, and who is standing in the middle. Three answers:

1

It goes under your account, on your key.

You bring your own API key from Anthropic or OpenAI. The agent’s requests are billed to you and governed by your agreement with them — not ours. You are their customer, not our product.

2

We are not in the middle of it.

Archie calls the AI company directly from your computer. Not relayed through us. Not proxied by us. We could not read it if we wanted to, because there is no Otian server in that path at all — there is nothing to read it with.

3

They don’t train on it.

Anthropic and OpenAI both state that they do not train their models on commercial API traffic. That is their commitment, not ours to make on their behalf — so don’t take our word for it. Read Anthropic’s commercial terms and OpenAI’s API data policy.

What running on your own machine actually buys you, then, is this: your life does not also accumulate on a server of ours. There is no Otian archive to be breached, subpoenaed, sold in an acquisition, or quietly repurposed when our business model changes. We didn’t promise not to look at your data. We built it so there is nothing to look at.

One more thing you didn’t ask. If your agent searches the web, that search runs on the AI provider’s infrastructure and is billed to your key — so your search query reaches their search backend, not just their model. Still not us.

Our Servers

Three Things. That’s The Whole List.

What we hold

  • Your email address, so you can sign in
  • Whether you own Archie
  • Which paid add-ons you’ve bought

What we don’t

  • Your prompts and your agent’s replies
  • Your files, notes, and knowledge base
  • Everything your agent remembers
  • Your calendar, emails, and messages
  • Your API keys
  • Which free add-ons you use

Not “encrypted and stored”. Not stored. We have no server that receives any of it, which is a much harder thing to get wrong than a policy about what we promise not to read.

Where We Fall Short

Every privacy page tells you what a company is good at. Here is the other half. If any of this changes, we will change it here on the same day.

Your agent asks before it acts — and it only helps if you read before you approve.

When your agent wants to change a calendar you’ve connected — create, move, or delete an event — or send an email, it doesn’t just do it. It proposes the exact action and carries it out only after you approve that specific one, enforced in the app itself rather than by politely instructing the AI. It proposes; you dispose.

Here is the honest limit that remains. The gate stops your agent from doing something you didn’t approve. It cannot stop it from proposing the wrong thing — if it misreads what you wanted and you approve without looking, it will carry out the mistake faithfully. Read what it hands you before you say yes, the way you’d check a draft before signing it.

Two things it still cannot do at all: it cannot spend money or buy anything — that ability doesn’t exist in the app — and it can only ever message you, never your team’s channels or anyone else.

Untrusted text can reach your agent — and the gate won’t fully fix it.

If your agent reads a Slack attachment, a meeting transcript, or a web page, that text lands in its context — and a hostile message could try to talk your agent into something you never asked for. This is a real class of attack against every AI agent, ours included, and we’re not going to pretend we’re exempt.

The approval gate stops such an attack from changing anything. It does not stop it from leaking something: a hostile instruction could still get your agent to run a web search whose query carries information from your conversation. We don’t have a complete answer to that yet. Nobody in this industry does. We’d rather write that sentence than let you assume we’d solved it.

Archie checks your license — but you own what you bought.

Archie is a one-time purchase, not a subscription. You buy it once and it’s yours — there’s nothing to cancel and nothing that lapses. The app does verify your license when you sign in, so it isn’t a fully offline product, and we’re not going to dress that up: our servers see that your account owns a license, and nothing more.

It’s worth being precise, because there are two scenarios companies love to blur. If you stop paying us, nothing happens to Archie — you already own it. If we disappear, it still keeps working. We’ve written it into our Terms: if Otian ever ceases operations, we publish a final version that needs no license check at all, within 30 days. It’s a term of the agreement, not a promise on a blog, so you don’t have to trust us for it.

What you’d lose if we vanished is updates, the marketplace, and support — the things a living company provides. You would not lose the agent you built. It’s on your disk, and it was never ours.

We keep a receipt for every paid add-on.

Buy a paid add-on and we store a permanent record: which one, how much, and when. It’s how we know to let you re-install it on a new machine. But it does mean we know which paid add-ons you own.

Free add-ons genuinely make no network call at all, so those we cannot see.

Nobody has audited us but us.

We have no third-party penetration test and no SOC 2 report. We’re two people, and we’re telling you that rather than hoping you don’t ask.

What we can offer instead is below: don’t trust us — check.

Don’t Trust Us

Check For Yourself. It Takes Ten Minutes.

Everything above this line is a claim, and claims are cheap. Here is how to prove or disprove ours without taking our word for a single thing.

1

Install a network monitor.

LuLu is free and open-source. Little Snitch is paid and friendlier. Either one shows you every connection an app on your Mac tries to make, and lets you block them.

2

Open Archie and use it properly.

Sign in. Install an add-on. Give your agent a real task — something with a file, or your calendar. Let it run. Then read the list of connections it made.

3

Here is exactly what you should see.

If you see anything else carrying your content, we have either lied to you or made a serious mistake, and we want to hear about it either way.

  • api.anthropic.com — or api.openai.com. Your agent thinking, on your key.
  • firestore.googleapis.com
    securetoken.googleapis.com — our sign-in and license check. Carries your account ID, nothing else.
  • otianai.com — once, checking for an update. Carries nothing about you.
  • github.com — only if an update is downloading.
  • Plus whichever accounts you connected — Google, Slack, Telegram, Discord, Fireflies. Directly, on your tokens.
4

Now block us, and watch nothing happen.

Add otianai.com to your firewall’s block list and keep working. Your agent carries on exactly as before. That is the entire argument on this page, reduced to something you can test in thirty seconds: we are not in the path, so cutting us out of it changes nothing.

Where we think this ends up. The real answer to “why should I believe you” is an open-source client, so that none of this rests on our word. We’re not there yet, and we’re not going to describe a plan as though it were a fact. For now: the monitor, the block list, and a company that would rather be corrected than trusted by default.

The Marketplace

An Add-on Is A Text File, Not A Program

The frightening version of a marketplace is: you install a stranger’s plugin, and it runs code on your computer, next to your email. That is not what this is — and the reason is structural, not a promise.

Archie has nowhere to run code. A Skill is a markdown file plus some settings. A Personality is a prompt. A Routine is a schedule. There is no scripting engine, no shell, and no way for an add-on to execute a program — so an add-on cannot read files off your disk on its own, and cannot ship your data to a stranger’s server. Not because we’d catch it. Because there is no mechanism for it to use.

What an add-on can do is tell your agent how to behave, and declare which of your connected accounts it needs. A calendar Skill gets calendar tools. Today that is coarser than we want: declaring “calendar” grants reading, creating, and deleting together, rather than asking you about each. Splitting those apart, and showing you in plain English what an add-on is asking for before you install it, is part of the same work as the approval gate above.

Every add-on is read by a human before it is listed. Right now that human is one of the two of us, and we read every line of every submission. That will not scale forever. When it stops being true, this paragraph will change — we won’t quietly leave it here.
